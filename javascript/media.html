A Song, a Road Trip, a Topic in AI

This page pairs the JEPA topic with two personal pieces of media — an original song I wrote called Snow Dreaming, and a short California travel video I filmed myself. Including them is partly because the assignment asks for an audio and a video element, and partly because reading about prediction in latent space is more enjoyable when something else is playing in the background.

Audio — Snow Dreaming (Original Song)

Snow Dreaming is a song I wrote and produced myself. The slow harmonic drift was my attempt at the suspended feeling of waiting for something you cannot quite see — which, on reflection, is also the loop a JEPA spends its training time in: holding a context, predicting a representation of what is hidden, then watching the actual answer arrive.

Video — California Road Trip

A short travel video from my own trip through California — coastline, highway, the kind of open light that makes you feel like the next turn is the answer to a question you forgot you asked. Filmed and edited by me, encoded as H.264 MP4 at 720p so it streams well across devices. A V-JEPA pretrained on enough video like this one would, in theory, learn the rough physics of camera motion, the way light shifts when you move, and the persistence of objects as they pass off-screen — without ever being told what any of those concepts are called.

Media Notes

  • Audio — original song by Yixia Yu; MP3 with M4A fallback for broader browser support
  • Video — personal travel footage; H.264 MP4 at 1280×720
  • Controls — native HTML5 controls attribute, no external player
  • Autoplay — disabled on purpose; visitors choose when to start

Why Include Media?

JEPA is a thesis about how a system perceives the world. Sound and motion are the cleanest examples of inputs where pixel-level prediction would be ridiculous and representation-level prediction is exactly what you want.
