The Problem with Predicting Pixels

The dominant approach to large-scale self-supervised learning today is generative: train a network to fill in missing pixels, missing words, missing patches. GPT predicts the next token; masked autoencoders reconstruct masked image patches. Yann LeCun, Meta’s Chief AI Scientist and a Turing Award laureate, has argued for several years that this is the wrong objective for building machines that understand the world. The reason is simple: most of the bits in an image are unpredictable noise, and forcing a model to predict them wastes capacity on detail that does not matter.

His proposed alternative is the Joint Embedding Predictive Architecture, or JEPA. Instead of predicting the raw input, a JEPA predicts an abstract representation of the missing part — a vector that captures only what is structurally predictable, throwing away the unpredictable details. The architecture has been instantiated as I-JEPA for images (2023), V-JEPA for video (2024), and V-JEPA 2 as a world model for robotics (2024).

Why This Topic

I picked JEPA as my topic of academic interest because it sits exactly at the boundary between two questions I keep returning to: what does it mean for a machine to understand something, and how much of intelligence is just good prediction at the right level of abstraction? LeCun’s position is unusual in the current landscape — he publicly disagrees with the “scale autoregressive LLMs and we’re done” narrative, and JEPA is the technical embodiment of that disagreement.

Two places to predict A generative model is trained to reconstruct the pixels of the missing region. A JEPA is trained to reconstruct the encoded representation of the missing region. The encoder is free to drop unpredictable noise, so capacity is spent only on the structure that actually transfers across examples.

Key Concepts

  • JEPA — Joint Embedding Predictive Architecture
  • Self-supervised — learns from raw data, no human labels
  • Representation space — abstract vectors, not pixels
  • World model — a system that can predict consequences of actions
