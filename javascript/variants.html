Three Generations: I-JEPA, V-JEPA, V-JEPA 2

The same architectural recipe has been instantiated in three increasingly ambitious settings. Each one applies the “predict in representation space” idea to a richer kind of input, and each one forces a slightly different design choice for what counts as context and target.

I-JEPA — Images (2023)

The first instantiation, published as “Self-Supervised Learning from Images with a Joint-Embedding Predictive Architecture” (CVPR 2023). I-JEPA samples a single large context block of an image and several large target blocks; the predictor is conditioned on a positional latent z that says where the target sits relative to the context. On standard image-classification benchmarks, I-JEPA beats pixel-reconstruction methods like MAE while training an order of magnitude faster.

V-JEPA — Video (2024)

V-JEPA extends the recipe to video, predicting representations of held-out spatio-temporal regions. Because video adds the time axis, the model implicitly learns about motion, object permanence, and short-horizon physics. Importantly, V-JEPA features transfer to action-recognition tasks without ever being trained on labels for actions.

V-JEPA 2 — A World Model for Robotics (2024)

V-JEPA 2 is the most ambitious step: a scaled-up V-JEPA used as a world model for an embodied agent. The model is fine-tuned with a small amount of robot-interaction data so that the predictor becomes action-conditioned, taking both the current representation and a candidate action and predicting the next representation. A planner then searches over actions to reach a goal. This is the JEPA recipe doing exactly what LeCun has been arguing for: predicting the future of the world in abstract form, then planning by simulating in latent space.

Generative vs. JEPA — Try the Switch

Most practitioners are introduced to self-supervised learning through generative methods (GPT, MAE, diffusion). JEPA is the same task framed differently. Click between the two buttons below to compare how each approach handles the same fill-in-the-blank problem on a video clip.

Generative: predict the missing pixels

The model receives the visible frames of the clip and is asked to render the missing future frames pixel-by-pixel. Loss is measured in pixel space — typically L2 or a reconstruction loss combined with a perceptual or adversarial term. Because so many futures are plausible (the leaves can sway any number of ways, the camera might pan slightly), the model is rewarded for hedging: the predicted frames look blurry or averaged, and a large fraction of the model’s capacity is consumed by texture and lighting that has nothing to do with what the clip is about.

JEPA: predict the missing representation

The same clip is fed in. The visible frames go through a context encoder; the missing frames go through an EMA target encoder. The predictor outputs a vector ŝ_y — its guess at the target encoder’s output — and the loss is just the L2 distance between ŝ_y and the actual s_y. Crucially, the encoder is free to throw away unpredictable noise during training, because there is no longer any pressure to reconstruct it. The features that survive are the ones that are consistently predictable across examples — exactly the structure you want a world model to capture.

How This Connects to LeCun’s Bigger Picture

JEPA is one piece of LeCun’s broader proposal for “Autonomous Machine Intelligence,” an architecture that includes a world model (the JEPA part), a configurator that sets goals, a short-term memory, and a planner that runs gradient-based search through the world model. In that picture, the JEPA is the perceptual and predictive backbone, and a hierarchical extension — H-JEPA — predicts at multiple time scales so the planner can reason over long horizons without exploding compute.

Variant Quick Reference

  • I-JEPA — images, large block targets, 2023
  • V-JEPA — video, spatio-temporal targets, 2024
  • V-JEPA 2 — world model for robotics, action-conditioned predictor, 2024
  • H-JEPA — hierarchical, multi-scale prediction (proposed)

Where JEPA Is Not (Yet) Ahead

Generative models are still better when the task is literal: image generation, text generation, anything where the user wants an actual artifact rather than a useful internal representation. JEPA is a representation-learning method first; the instantiations that look most exciting (V-JEPA 2) appear when those representations are wired into a planner.
